1. What is a percentage, really?

Forget the exam for a second. Imagine you have a chocolate bar broken into exactly 100 tiny pieces — every bar, no matter how big or small, gets cut into 100 equal pieces. That's the whole trick behind percentages.

If your friend eats 27 of those 100 pieces, we say your friend ate 27 percent of the bar. "Percent" literally comes from the Latin per centum, meaning "out of a hundred." That's it. That's the entire idea. Every time you see a % sign, your brain should just read it as "out of 100".

So when someone says "this shirt is 40% off," they mean: imagine the price cut into 100 equal parts, and 40 of those parts are being removed. When a teacher says "you scored 72%," they mean: imagine the full marks split into 100 equal parts, and you collected 72 of them — no matter if the test was actually out of 50 or 500.

This is exactly why percentages are so useful: they let you compare two completely different things (a 50-mark test and a 500-mark test) on the same fair scale of 100.

2. Seeing it: the 100-box picture

Here are 100 little boxes. I've coloured in 27 of them — exactly the chocolate example from above.

27 filled out of 100 boxes  =  27 / 100  =  27%

Now here's the part most people never get told clearly: real life almost never hands you a "100" to work with. A class might have 35 students, a salary might be ₹48,000, a town might have 7,200 people. So your real job in every percentage question is just this:

shrink or stretch whatever number you have so it behaves like it's "out of 100."

That single sentence is 90% of the percentage chapter. Everything else is just different ways of asking you to do that shrinking/stretching.

3. Fraction ↔ Decimal ↔ Percentage

A percentage is just a fraction with 100 fixed as the bottom number (denominator). Because of that, you can always slide between three forms of the same value:

5. Core formulas (highlight these)

Every single percentage question — no matter how scary it looks — is built from one of these blocks.

the master formula
Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100
finding the part
Part = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Whole
finding the whole
Whole = Part ÷ (Percentage ÷ 100)
percentage increase
% Increase = (Increase ÷ Original) × 100
percentage decrease
% Decrease = (Decrease ÷ Original) × 100
value after a% increase
New Value = Original × (100 + a)/100
value after a% decrease
New Value = Original × (100 − a)/100
two changes, one after another
Net % = a + b + (ab/100)

A quick note on that last one, the successive change formula: use a + sign for an increase and a − sign for a decrease inside the formula. So "20% increase then 10% decrease" is a=+20, b=−10 → 20 + (−10) + (20×−10)/100 = 20 − 10 − 2 = +8% net increase. We'll unpack exactly why this works in the next subtopic (Successive Percentage Change) — for now just know it exists.

6. Worked examples, step by step

Five examples, getting progressively closer to real exam difficulty. Read the reasoning, not just the arithmetic.

Example 1 — the absolute basics

Q: What is 25% of 80?

25% means 25 out of every 100. So we want 25/100 of 80.

25% of 80 = (25/100) × 80 = 0.25 × 80 = 20

Shortcut: 25% = 1/4 (from our table), so just do 80 ÷ 4 = 20.

Answer: 20

Example 2 — "what percent" (reverse direction)

Q: 45 is what percent of 180?

Here 45 is the "Part" and 180 is the "Whole." Use the master formula.

Percentage = (45 ÷ 180) × 100 = 0.25 × 100 = 25%

Answer: 25%

Example 3 — percentage increase

Q: A shopkeeper's daily sale went from ₹400 to ₹460. Find the percentage increase.

Increase = 460 − 400 = 60. Original (the number we're comparing against) = 400.

% Increase = (60 ÷ 400) × 100 = 15%

Before (₹400) vs After (₹460), shown to scale
₹400
₹460

Answer: 15% increase

Example 4 — the "more than" trap

Q: A is 25% more than B. By what percentage is B less than A?

This is the single most common beginner mistake in the whole chapter — students assume the answer is also 25%. It is NOT, because the base (the "whole" we divide by) changes.

Let B = 100. "A is 25% more than B" → A = 100 + 25 = 125.

Now find how much less B is than A, as a percentage of A (since now A is our base):

% less = ((125 − 100) ÷ 125) × 100 = (25 ÷ 125) × 100 = 20%

Answer: 20% (not 25%) — always re-check which number is the new base.

Example 5 — building toward exam level

Q: In an election with two candidates, the winner got 60% of the total votes and won by 4,800 votes. Find the total number of votes.

If winner has 60%, loser has the remaining 40%. The "win margin" is the difference between these two shares.

Margin % = 60% − 40% = 20% of total votes = 4,800.

Total = 4800 ÷ (20/100) = 4800 × 5 = 24,000

Answer: 24,000 total votes

7. Mistakes that cost marks

✗ Mistake 1

Thinking "A is 25% more than B" and "B is 25% less than A" are the same number. They never are (see Example 4) — the base changes between the two statements.

✗ Mistake 2

Believing a 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease brings you back to the original value. It does not — you end up at 96% of the original, a 4% net loss. (Check it with the successive-change formula from Section 5.)

✗ Mistake 3

Mixing up "percentage" with "percentage point." If interest goes from 8% to 10%, that's a rise of 2 percentage points, but a 25% increase in the rate itself (2÷8)×100. SSC sometimes tests this exact distinction in GA, and increasingly in mixed Quant statements.

✗ Mistake 4

Forgetting to re-anchor the base in multi-step problems. Once a value changes, the next percentage almost always applies to the new value, not the original one — unless the question explicitly says otherwise.

A person's monthly expenses are 66⅔% more than savings. Income rises 44%, expenses rise 60%, and savings increase by ₹1,040. Find the initial expenditure.

Solution
66⅔% more means expenses = (5/3) × savings. Take savings = 3x, expenses = 5x, income = 8x. New income = 8x × 1.44 = 11.52x. New expenses = 5x × 1.6 = 8x. New savings = 11.52x − 8x = 3.52x. Increase = 3.52x − 3x = 0.52x = 1040 → x = 2000. Initial expenditure = 5x = 10,000

9. One-page revision summary

  • % always means "out of 100" — your job is to scale any number onto that 100-scale.
  • Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100
  • Part = (% ÷ 100) × Whole  |  Whole = Part ÷ (% ÷ 100)
  • New value after +a% = Original × (100+a)/100
  • New value after −a% = Original × (100−a)/100
  • Two changes back to back: Net% = a + b + (ab/100), with sign for increase/decrease
  • "A is x% more than B" ≠ "B is x% less than A" — always re-anchor the base
  • Know your fraction-percentage table cold: 1/3, 1/6, 1/7, 1/8, 1/12 etc. — this is where SSC speed is won or lost
